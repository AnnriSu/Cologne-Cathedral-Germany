Church-Architecture

The Cologne Cathedral, dedicated to St. Peter and the Virgin Mary, was designed in the tradition of the High Gothic cathedrals of northern France.

Its structure is richly detailed, though somewhat formal in execution, reflecting an almost scientific precision in both design and craftsmanship. The cathedral stretches 144.6 meters in length and rises to about 45 meters in height.

While its ground plan draw clear inspiration from Amiens Cathedral, it also incorporates distinctive features that set it apart from its French counterparts.



Taken and Submitted by James Palik on Unesco.org

The Cologne Cathedral features an eight-bay nave and a four-bay choir, each designed with double aisles—the outer aisles slightly narrower than the inner ones. Both arms of the transept contain four bays with aisles on the east and west sides. The two west towers, each built over four aisle bays, are larger and deeper than those in French cathedrals, resulting in their impressive height.

At the eastern end, the chevet includes an ambulatory with seven radiating chapels, and the entire structure is covered with quadripartite rib vaults. While the elevation and cross-section resemble Amiens Cathedral, Cologne’s design uses slimmer and more numerous vertical supports, creating a more intricate look. The arcade piers have molded surfaces and small foliage capitals, and the vault shafts extend seamlessly upward. The triforium and clerestory are joined by continuous mullions, each bay containing four windows. The overall height matches Amiens, but the triforium is positioned lower, dividing the elevation in a 3:1:3 ratio.



Taken and Submitted by Amos Chapple on Unesco.org

Architectural details such as tracery patterns, pierced parapets, gabled windows, and flying buttresses also mirror the French Gothic style.

The 4-meter-high parchment drawing of the west façade guided 19th-century builders during restoration. The lower section reflects the nave and aisles, with square lower towers that become octagonal higher up, ending in openwork spires topped with large finials.

Unlike the west façade, the transept façades had no medieval plans and were newly designed by architect Ernst Friedrich Zwirner in the Gothic Revival style, modeled after the west front.

Between 1880 and 1900, mosaic flooring was added to the choir, made from ceramic tiles produced in Mettlach based on designs by August Ottmar von Essenwein. These mosaics feature mythological and allegorical scenes, enriching the cathedral’s artistic interior.

(Architecture of Cologne Cathedral - Cologne Cathedral, 2025)
